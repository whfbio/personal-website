## 🔬 Research Directions

The Wang Lab investigates the molecular and cellular mechanisms that regulate heart development, maturation, disease, and regeneration. Our research integrates developmental biology, chromatin biology, single-cell genomics, spatial transcriptomics, stem-cell models, and in vivo functional genomics. Current research directions include:

### 🫀 1. Regulatory mechanisms of cardiomyocyte maturation
We investigate how chromatin architecture, epigenetic regulation, transcriptional programs, metabolism, and local tissue signals coordinate cardiomyocyte maturation after birth. Our goal is to identify the regulatory mechanisms that control structural, electrophysiological, and metabolic maturation and determine how these mechanisms become disrupted in pediatric heart disease.

### 🧭 2. Spatial organization and cell-cell communication in the developing heart
Heart development depends on coordinated interactions among cardiomyocytes, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, immune cells, and other cardiac cell populations. We combine single-cell and spatial multi-omics with in vivo perturbation approaches to define how local cellular interactions regulate heart development, maturation, disease progression, and repair.

### 🧩 3. Functional interpretation of congenital heart disease genes and variants
Many genes and non-coding variants associated with congenital heart disease remain functionally unexplained. Working closely with clinicians, geneticists, computational biologists, and cardiovascular researchers at CHOP and Penn, we will systematically test clinically identified genes and variants using CRISPR-based perturbation, single-cell and spatial readouts, human induced pluripotent stem cells, and vascularized cardiac organoids.

Our long-term goal is to build a functional bridge between human genetic discoveries and mechanistic cardiovascular biology and to identify pathways that can be targeted therapeutically.

### 🔁 4. Direct cardiac reprogramming and heart regeneration
We investigate how cell identity can be remodeled to promote cardiac repair. Using MGT/AM reprogramming cocktail, we aim to directly convert cardiac fibroblasts into induced cardiomyocyte-like cells.

Potential projects will focus on improving the efficiency and maturity of induced cardiomyocytes. Single-cell and spatial multi-omics will be used to track reprogramming trajectories, identify intermediate cell states, and uncover regulatory mechanisms that can improve cardiac regeneration.

## 🏛️ Lab Introduction

The Wang lab combines chromatin biology, single-cell and spatial genomics, stem-cell models, and in vivo functional genomics to study cardiovascular development and disease. The early focus of the lab will be defining chromatin landscape and cell communications that control postnatal cardiomyocyte maturation and on developing scalable approaches to functionally test regulatory genes directly in vivo.

The Wang Lab will expand these approaches into a broader cardiovascular functional-genomics platform spanning cardiomyocytes, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and immune cells. By combining mechanistic studies with clinically driven genetic discoveries, the laboratory aims to uncover causal pathways underlying congenital and pediatric heart disease and identify new opportunities for cardiac regeneration and therapy.

The laboratory will be affiliated with the **Children's Hospital of Philadelphia, the University of Pennsylvania, and the Cardiovascular Institute**. The CHOP/Penn research community provides a highly collaborative environment with extensive expertise in pediatric cardiology, human genetics, developmental biology, stem-cell biology, genomics, bioengineering, computational biology, and translational medicine.
